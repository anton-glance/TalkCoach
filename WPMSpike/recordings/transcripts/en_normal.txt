So I've been thinking about how I spend my time during the workday, and honestly, it's kind of a mess. I start the morning planning to focus on deep work, you know, the kind of stuff that actually matters, and then by ten thirty I'm three meetings deep and I haven't written a single line of code. It's frustrating because I know what I should be doing. I just keep getting pulled into other things.
Yeah.Last week I tried something different. I blocked off the first two hours of every morning, no meetings, no Slack, nothing. And I have to say, it actually worked pretty well. I shipped more in those ten hours than I had in the previous two weeks combined. The hard part is defending that time when other people want it. My manager was fine with it, but a few teammates pushed back, basically saying we need to be available for collaboration. Which, fair enough, but availability all the time means availability for nothing in particular.
I think the real lesson here is that being responsive feels productive but isn't. Like, answering a message in two minutes feels great, but if I do that fifty times a day, I've spent maybe an hour just context-switching. So my plan for next month is to keep the morning blocks, add a midday review, and basically treat afternoons as the meeting zone. We'll see if it sticks.
